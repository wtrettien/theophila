The first canto ends with this pattern poem in the shape of an altar, thereby setting the stage — in a literal-minded way — for Theophila’s sacrifice. Part of the deep history of concrete poetry, such shaped verse was not uncommon in the early seventeenth century. Joshua Sylvester’s 1604 translation of Guillaume du Bartas’s Divine Weeks and Works turns dedicatory poems to the muses into altars, for instance, while George Herbert’s The Temple (1633) famously contains two pattern poems, “Easter Wings” and “The Altar,” seen here in a digital facsimile from the Folger Shakespeare Library.
