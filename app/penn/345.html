By the time Theophila appeared, though, the fad for pattern poetry had begun to fade. Of the two recycled plates with religious pattern poems that Benlowes adds to the end of the book, as seen here, one is dated 1632, twenty years before the book now containing them was published. (View this date by clicking on the verso to zoom in, then navigating to the bottom right corner.) Thus his use of shaped verse must have appeared to contemporaneous readers as almost a throwback to an earlier moment, recalling Samuel Butler's mockery of Benlowes as an eccentric coot obsessed with “Altars and Pyramids in Poetry,” writing, “he has out-done all Men that Way.”
